# Piece 1 — Setup & framing

## The model we are going to fit

We assume each exam score `y` is generated by:

$$y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i + \varepsilon_i, \qquad \varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$$

Three unknown parameters: `β₀` (intercept), `β₁` (slope), `σ²` (noise variance). The whole project is about estimating these three numbers and asking "how sure are we?"

## Population vs sample (videos 1, 2, 3, 5)

- The **population** is the imaginary infinite collection of all possible (hours, score) pairs that could be produced by this model. The true `β₀, β₁, σ²` are properties of that population — fixed, unknown, unreachable.
- Our 8 points are a **sample** drawn from this population.
- From the sample we compute **estimates** `β̂₀, β̂₁, σ̂²`. Hats mean "estimated from data."
- Different samples → different estimates. The estimates are themselves random variables (this is the seed of error analysis later).

## Why we pick Normal noise

Two reasons, both important:

1. **CLT justification (videos 6, 8).** Real noise is the sum of many small unrelated effects — sleep, mood, distractions, room temperature, lucky guesses. The Central Limit Theorem says the sum of many independent small effects looks Gaussian. So `ε ~ Normal` is not arbitrary — it follows from how noise is built in real life.
2. **Mathematical convenience.** This assumption is what will make MLE collapse into clean closed-form formulas in Piece 3. With other noise distributions we'd need numerical optimization.

## The IID assumption

We assume the noise terms `ε₁, ε₂, ..., ε₈` are **independent** (one student's bad day doesn't affect another's) and **identically distributed** (every student has the same `σ²`). Same i.i.d. setup Brunton uses everywhere.

## What the picture is telling us

The points fall almost on a straight line, with a small wobble around it. That wobble is exactly the `εᵢ` term. Our job in Pieces 2–4 will be to find the line that "best explains" this cloud of points, where "best" means: **the line that makes the observed wobble most likely under our Gaussian noise assumption** — i.e. MLE.

You can already eyeball the answer: the line goes from about `y ≈ 47` at `x = 0` and rises by about `5.5` for each extra hour. So mentally `β̂₀ ≈ 47`, `β̂₁ ≈ 5.5`. Keep these eyeball numbers in mind — when we do MLE in Piece 3, the formulas should land near them.
